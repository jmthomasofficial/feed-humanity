## The Context

Jensen Huang announced AGI in January 2026. The world heard it. 318 million people are currently facing hunger. These two facts exist simultaneously, and the distance between them is the most important story in technology right now.

Feed Humanity is the bridge. Not symbolically — operationally. The logistics problem of matching food surplus to food deficit at city and national scale is exactly the problem that modern AI is built to solve. Route optimization, demand prediction, multi-variable matching with time constraints, real-time inventory management — this is not a stretch for AI systems that can write code and pass bar exams. This is table stakes.

The ask is in three parts: the personal act, the engineering contribution, and the institutional commitment. They are independent. A CEO can do the first without the company doing the other two. But together, they create a proof of concept for beneficial AI that no demo, no paper, and no press release can match.

## Part 2: Engineering Resource Deployment

The Feed Humanity AI infrastructure is open-source. Your team can contribute to a system that will, if this works at scale, optimize the distribution of billions of pounds of food that are currently being wasted.

**Surplus matching algorithm contributions:**
The core matching engine (`ai-dispatch/matching_engine.py`) uses a distance-and-perishability-weighted scoring system. Improvements that would move the needle:
- Multi-stop route optimization (currently handles single matches; needs to chain multiple pickups and deliveries efficiently)
- Perishability prediction by food type (baked goods: 4 hours; cooked proteins: 2 hours; dry goods: indefinite)
- Demand prediction by location, day of week, and weather
- Integration APIs for existing food rescue platforms (Too Good To Go, Flashfood, OLIO)

Any team that has built recommendation systems, logistics optimization, or real-time matching systems has directly transferable skills. The codebase is Python, the architecture is documented in `ai-dispatch/`, and the test suite is in `ai-dispatch/test_dispatch.py`.

**Platform development funding:**
The live impact dashboard — the global map showing every verified meal, the city leaderboards, the real-time counter — requires infrastructure that scales with viral adoption. When this campaign reaches millions of posts, the data pipeline needs to handle it. A $100,000 infrastructure contribution funds the database, compute, and API capacity to handle global scale.

This is a public benefit platform. The code is MIT licensed. The impact data is public. Your logo on the infrastructure is a permanent record of who helped build the system that organized the world's largest feeding movement.

**Volunteer engineering hours:**
Three engineers for three months can build the full AI playbook generator: the tool that takes a zip code, a budget, and available time, and outputs a personalized action plan for participating in the campaign. This is a solved problem technically — it is a recommendation system with local data. The social impact of making participation trivially easy for every individual is incalculable.

## Part 3: Institutional Commitment

**The format that works:**
Specific. Measurable. Time-bounded.

"We are committing to fund 500,000 meals through the Feed Humanity campaign before the end of Q2 2026, using AI-optimized surplus matching to maximize food recovery from our campus cafeterias and partner restaurants."

That is a press release. That is an ESG milestone. That is a challenge to every other tech company in the sector.

**What "organized by AI" means:**
The meals funded through the dispatch system are not just charitable donations. They are proof-of-concept data points. Each matched supply-to-demand pair is a data entry in a growing dataset showing that AI logistics can meaningfully address food insecurity at scale. By Q3, if ten companies are using the dispatch system, the aggregate data shows AI-organized food recovery across major US cities. That dataset is a research paper. That paper is evidence in the "AGI for good" argument that will define the next decade of AI regulation and public trust.

**The co-branding position:**

Not in a vague, aspirational sense — in the sense that historical records will note what the most powerful technology companies in history did with their capability when the opportunity to prove its benefit was explicit and public.

The ones that did not participate will be asked why. That question, asked for years in shareholder meetings and Senate hearings and technology journalism, is the alternative.
