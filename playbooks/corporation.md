# Tier 5: The Corporation — $5,000 to $100,000+

You have infrastructure, reach, and the ability to move at scale. This playbook shows you how to deploy that capacity in a way that generates real impact, genuine employee engagement, measurable ESG outcomes, and brand positioning that no advertising spend can replicate.

---

## The Employee Challenge

The most effective corporate activation starts internally. Challenge your own employees before you challenge anyone else.

**Structure:**
Launch an internal "Feed Humanity Challenge" across your company. Any employee who participates — individually, as a team, or by organizing a workplace food drive — gets their act featured in the company's social content and logged in the global campaign counter.

Make it a competition between offices, departments, or teams. "Which office feeds the most people this month?" Corporate competitive dynamics are real and they work. The same instinct that drives sales team leaderboards drives charity competitions. Use it.

**How to set it up:**
1. Send a company-wide email from a VP or C-suite executive. Leadership participation signals that this is real, not a box-checking exercise.
2. Create a simple reporting mechanism: a form, a Slack channel, or a dedicated email address where employees log their participation.
3. Set a time window: 2 weeks to 1 month works well.
4. Track by team or office and publish a leaderboard on your internal communications channel.
5. Announce the winning team and donate to a food bank in their name at the close.

**Content that emerges from this:**
Employees doing the challenge individually generate authentic, unscripted social content that outperforms any brand-produced campaign. When an employee in your Chicago office posts their Feed Run with their coworkers, tags their company, and it gets 40,000 views — that is earned media you cannot buy.

## The 2:1 Matching Program

Match every employee food donation at 2 to 1. This is the most efficient corporate lever in this playbook.

**How it works:**
Employee donates $20 worth of food or cash to a verified food organization. Corporation contributes $40 in matching food purchasing or cash to the same or a partner organization. Every employee dollar becomes three dollars of impact.

**Why 2:1 instead of 1:1:**
The 2:1 match is unusual enough to be remarkable. "Our company matches 2 to 1" is a more compelling communication to employees than "we match dollar for dollar." The difference in actual cost is modest; the difference in perceived generosity is significant. 2:1 matching drives higher participation rates.

**Logistics:**
- Designate a program administrator to track matching claims.
- Set a monthly or quarterly cap that fits your budget. Even a $5,000 cap still represents real impact.
- Use Benevity, Submittable, or a simple spreadsheet system to track employee donations.
- Publish the running match total on your internal channels throughout the drive.

## Regional Event Sponsorship

Feed Humanity events happen at the neighborhood level — food drives, Feed Runs, community meals. You can sponsor these events without running them yourself.

**What sponsorship looks like:**
- Fund the food purchasing for a community Feed Run ($200 to $500)
- Provide branded #FeedHumanity event supplies (boxes, bags, posters) to organizers in your region
- Cover the logistics costs for a food bank event (trucks, fuel, volunteer coordination)
- Sponsor a "Feed Humanity Day" at a university or school in your market

**What you get:**
- Your brand name and logo in the event content
- Recognition on the Feed Humanity partner directory
- Co-branded social content that your marketing team can share
- The ability to direct employee volunteers to events you've sponsored, connecting the internal challenge to external impact

**Finding events to sponsor:**
Check the Feed Humanity community board (on the website) for upcoming events in your operating regions. Contact organizers directly. Most Tier 3 organizers have more motivation than budget — a $300 sponsorship transforms a modest food drive into a significant community event.

## ESG and CSR Reporting Integration

Feed Humanity participation generates documentation that feeds directly into ESG and CSR frameworks.

**What you can report:**
- Total meals funded through matching programs (quantifiable impact)
- Employee volunteer hours (Feed Runs, event participation)
- Food waste diverted through the surplus donation program if your business also has a restaurant/catering component
- Community investment in local food security

**How to document it:**
1. Log all company participation in the Feed Humanity partner portal on the website. Your aggregate impact is tracked there.
2. Collect food bank receipts for any direct donations — these show weight, item count, and recipient organization.
3. Track employee participation through your internal reporting mechanism.
4. Request a campaign impact certificate from the Feed Humanity organization at the end of the program period.

**Reporting frameworks this aligns with:**
- GRI 203: Indirect Economic Impacts (community investment)
- GRI 413: Local Communities
- SASB Consumer Goods sector: Community Development
- UN SDGs 2 (Zero Hunger) and 17 (Partnerships)
- B Corp Community Impact assessment

Your sustainability team will know how to fold this documentation into your existing reporting structure. Feed Humanity provides the data; your team contextualizes it within your ESG narrative.

## Co-Branded Content Strategy

Corporate participation generates significant content opportunities when executed correctly.

**Announce the commitment first:**
Issue a press release or corporate social post announcing your Feed Humanity commitment. Specific numbers work better than vague pledges. "We are committing to fund 10,000 meals through the Feed Humanity campaign over the next 60 days" is a story. "We support the Feed Humanity initiative" is not.

**Document the activation:**
Content from your employee challenge, from sponsored events, from matching milestone moments — all of this is co-branded content. "[Company] x #FeedHumanity" frames every piece as both your brand story and the campaign story.

**The challenge to competitors:**
After your first content post, tag three competitor companies publicly. "We're in. @[Competitor1] @[Competitor2] @[Competitor3] — your turn." Corporate competitive dynamics mean that once one company in a sector commits publicly, the others face pressure to match or exceed. This is the same dynamic that made corporate Pride commitments ubiquitous — but with measurable food impact instead of logo colors.

**Milestone posts:**
As your programs run, post at milestones. "We just passed 1,000 meals. Halfway to our goal." "Our Chicago office led this week's leaderboard. 340 meals." Real numbers, real progress, real story.

**The employee story:**
With permission, feature individual employee stories. A team that ran a Saturday Feed Run together. An employee who organized the first food drive in their neighborhood because of the company program. An operations manager who connected the surplus from the company cafeteria to a local shelter. These human-scale stories inside a large company are the most emotionally resonant content you can produce.

## Paid Time Off to Participate

Give employees paid time to do this. One afternoon. One Saturday morning with an optional team event. The cost is modest. The signal is enormous: this company actually means it.

When leadership visibly participates — when the VP shows up to the Feed Run, when the office manager organizes the food drive and the CEO posts about it — participation rates across the company multiply. People follow demonstrated priorities, not stated ones.

**Structuring the PTO benefit formally:**

Employees should not have to ask permission in a way that feels awkward or uncertain. Write the policy explicitly:

> "[Company] provides each full-time employee one paid day per quarter for participation in a documented Feed Humanity activity. To use this benefit, submit a brief activity description to your manager at least two business days in advance. After the activity, submit a short completion note — what you did, where, and the outcome — via the internal form. Activities are logged in our impact tracking system."

The approval default should be yes. Any denial requires written justification submitted to HR. This framing shifts the social dynamic: participation is the expected norm, not the exception.

**How to track and report it:**

Maintain a central log: employee ID or name, date, activity type, location, and documented outcome (food bank receipt number, pounds, meals). Export this quarterly for ESG reporting. Share the aggregate internally: "This quarter, our employees collectively dedicated X days to Feed Humanity activities across Y cities." That number, shared at the all-hands, is the proof that the company means what it says.

## Public Challenge: Challenge Three Competitor Companies

After your first public commitment post, challenge three competitor companies by name. This is the most powerful growth mechanic at the corporate tier.

**Why it works:** No communications team can advise their company to publicly decline to feed people when challenged in front of a shared customer base. The asymmetry is total. The cost of participating is low. The cost of visibly declining is high. Most companies, when named, find a way to say yes.

**The exact post format:**

> "We just committed to fund [X meals / X events / $X] for the #FeedHumanity campaign this quarter. We're challenging [Company A], [Company B], and [Company C] to make their own specific commitments. Every company in this industry has the capacity. The question is whether we use it. [Tag each company's main account] #FeedHumanity"

**Who to challenge:**
- Your top three direct competitors in your primary market
- Sector peers of similar scale who have been publicly active on ESG topics (they have already signaled alignment and are more likely to convert)
- Companies whose leadership you know personally — a direct CEO-to-CEO call before the public post dramatically increases the conversion rate

**After they accept:** Amplify their participation. Repost their commitment. "Great to see [Company] joining. Together we're at [combined total] meals this quarter." Making them look good after they join recruits the next wave of companies who see the positive social dynamic.

## Specific Measurable Commitments Template

Vague corporate commitments erode trust. Specific commitments build it and create accountability that forces organizational action.

---

**[Company Name] — Feed Humanity Commitment Statement**

Campaign period: [Start Date] through [End Date]

**Meals facilitated:** We commit to organizing or funding a minimum of [X] meals through company-sponsored activities, employee Feed Runs, and matched donation programs during this period.

**Employee participation:** We will activate [X] employees across [X] offices in documented Feed Humanity activities, providing one paid day per employee for this purpose.

**Matching program:** We are committing up to $[X] in 2:1 matched employee donations to Feeding America partner food banks during this campaign window. The match is open to all full-time employees from [start date] through [end date].

**Events sponsored:** We will fund [X] regional Feed Humanity events in the following markets: [list cities].

**Challenge issued:** We are publicly challenging [Company A], [Company B], and [Company C] to publish their own specific commitments by [date — recommend 2 weeks from this post].

**Accountability:** We will publish our actual results — meals delivered, dollars matched, events funded — by [date — recommend within 2 weeks of campaign close].

Signed: [CEO Name], [Title] — [Date]

---

Every blank in this template requires a real number before you publish it. If a number feels uncomfortable to commit to publicly, set it lower until it feels achievable and then publish it. Under-promise and over-deliver publicly is far better than a vague statement that generates no accountability.

## The PR Pitch in Corporate Language

This is the verbatim pitch your communications team sends to journalists, and the language your CEO uses in the announcement.

---

**Subject: [Company Name] commits to [X] meals through the Feed Humanity campaign — [Date]**

[Company Name] today announced a commitment to fund and facilitate [X] meals through the Feed Humanity campaign, a global open-source movement organizing food distribution in response to rising food insecurity during the AGI transition period.

The commitment includes: a 2:1 employee donation matching program capped at $[X], paid time off for [X] employees to participate in community food activities, and sponsorship of [X] regional Feed Humanity events across [list markets].

"The companies that show up for their communities during periods of economic disruption are the ones that earn lasting trust," said [CEO Name]. "Feed Humanity gave us a specific, measurable way to act on that belief. We committed to real numbers and we will publish real results."

The Feed Humanity campaign is an open-source, nonprofit-aligned initiative running in [X] countries. Participation is tracked through a public data repository. [Company Name]'s contribution will be documented and publicly verifiable.

[Company Name] has also challenged [Company A], [Company B], and [Company C] to publish their own specific commitments by [date].

Results will be published at [company website or campaign profile] by [date].

For media inquiries: [contact]

---

The companies that move first in their sector get the story. The companies that move second get a mention. The companies that do not move get asked why — in earnings calls, in shareholder letters, and in the journalism that will document this period for the next decade.

The choice is not whether to participate. The choice is how visibly and how early.
