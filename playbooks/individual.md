# Tier 1: The Individual — $5 to $50, 15 Minutes

You have a few bucks and a phone. That is enough. This is the atomic unit of the entire campaign — everything else is just this act at greater scale.

---

## The Complete Checklist

- [ ] **Buy a meal for someone who looks like they could use one.**
  Walk to the nearest fast food counter, grocery store, bodega, or food truck. Get something real — a hot sandwich, a full meal combo, a bag of groceries, a coffee and pastry, a bag of chips. Anything edible counts. You do not need to spend more than $5. You do not need to spend anything at all if you're cooking.

- [ ] **Give it to them.**
  Hand it directly to a person. If they're camera-shy, step to the side for the handoff and film your own face or just the food. If they genuinely do not want to be filmed, respect that immediately — a selfie with the meal before the handoff works fine. The act still counts. The food still gets eaten.

- [ ] **Film the moment.**
  You do not need a production crew. One phone, held naturally. The handoff, the smile, the reaction — that is the content. Even 15 seconds is enough. If nothing is filmable, a photo of the meal in your hand, about to be given, is sufficient.

- [ ] **Post it with `#FeedHumanity` + your city.**
  Example: `#FeedHumanity #FeedHumanityNashville`
  Short caption works fine. "Fed a stranger. Your turn." is enough. The hashtag does the heavy lifting.

- [ ] **Challenge three people by name.**
  Tag them directly: `@person1 @person2 @person3 — your turn.`
  That is the entire challenge mechanic. The social pressure is positive — who is going to publicly decline feeding someone? Tag friends, coworkers, family, people you know want to do good things. Tag local celebrities or accounts if you feel bold.

- [ ] **Optional: Log it on the Feed Humanity site.**
  Add to the global meal counter at [jmthomasofficial.com/feedhumanity](https://jmthomasofficial.com/feedhumanity). Takes 30 seconds. Every logged meal moves the counter.

## What Counts

This is not a strict checklist of approved food types. If someone is hungry and you gave them something to eat, it counts.

Specific examples:
- Fast food (McDonald's, Taco Bell, any chain, any local spot)
- Vending machine items (a protein bar and a drink for someone outside)
- Grocery bags dropped at a food bank
- Cooking a batch of something at home and bringing it to a shelter
- Coffee and a pastry for a construction worker
- Pizza left over from your office, delivered to a shelter instead of the trash
- A bag of produce from your garden

The only thing that does not count: a donation to a fund. This campaign is about physical food changing hands.

## Pro Tips

**Construction crews and overnight workers are underserved targets.**
Road crews, warehouse workers, overnight hospital staff, hotel housekeeping, 24-hour store clerks — these people work physically demanding jobs, often with no break room, no food perks, and no random acts of kindness. Walking up to a job site with 10 sandwiches generates immediate, genuine, emotional reactions. These are the clips that go viral because the gratitude is absolutely real.

**Food bank drop-offs multiply your impact.**
A $20 bag of dried beans, canned goods, and rice at a food bank becomes 8 to 15 meals after distribution. You do not need to identify an individual recipient. The food bank staff know who needs it. Film yourself walking in with the bag and handing it to the intake person — that handoff shot is clean and powerful.

**Cook a batch.**
If you cook, double the recipe and bring the extra half to a neighbor in need, a local shelter, or a community meal program. The cost is almost nothing. A pot of soup that feeds 12 people costs about the same as feeding two people at a restaurant.

**Early morning is when the content is most powerful.**
The breakfast handoff — coffee and food to someone who slept outside, or to a worker showing up before sunrise — hits different emotionally than midday content. The light is better too.

**You do not need to be on camera.**
If you're private, just film the meal from above (a bird's-eye shot of the food you're about to give), then a wide shot of the handoff from behind. Your face never has to appear. The act is the story.

Add your city: `#FeedHumanityNashville` or `#FeedHumanityChicago` or `#FeedHumanityLondon`

Add 1-2 of these supporting tags based on what fits your post:
- `#FeedTheNeed` — good for urgency-focused posts
- `#OneMealChallenge` — good for challenge-mechanic focused posts
- `#AGIForGood` — good if your caption touches on the tech angle
- `#FeedForward` — good if you're emphasizing the chain reaction

Do not stack 10 hashtags. The primary tag is the only one that matters for counting. Extra tags help discoverability. Keep the caption clean.

## FAQ

**What if they do not want to be filmed?**
Respect it immediately. Step back, do not press. You can still film the food before you hand it over, or film yourself walking away after. The act counts regardless of documentation. It is better to have an undocumented good act than a documented uncomfortable interaction.

**What if I cannot afford it right now?**
Then do not do it. This challenge never asks anyone to go into debt or skip their own meals. If $5 is a stretch this week, share someone else's post instead, or come back next week. The challenge scales with what you have. Zero-dollar participation is also real: cook extra at home, donate something from your pantry, drive someone to a food bank.

**What if the person refuses the food?**
That happens. Some people have reasons — pride, allergies, distrust of strangers, they already ate. Say "no problem" and move on. Find someone else, or drop the food with a shelter. Do not film someone refusing. Do not make a big deal of a rejection.

**Does it have to be a stranger?**
No. A neighbor who is going through a hard time counts. A coworker who you know skips lunch to make rent counts. A family member who needs help counts. The spirit is feeding someone who needs it. You know who those people are.

**Do I have to challenge three specific people?**
The mechanic works better when you tag real people, but a general "who's next?" at the end of your post still spreads the idea. Tag people if you can — the nomination pressure is part of the exponential growth engine.

**What if nobody responds to my challenge?**
Do it again. The first few posts from anyone rarely land. Most viral moments are the fifth or sixth attempt. Tag different people next time. Try a different time of day. The act itself is complete whether or not anyone responds.

Every single meal in this campaign is a data point. The AI tracker reads your `#FeedHumanity` posts, counts meals, maps locations, and adds to the live global counter. Your one meal in Nashville or London or Nairobi is part of a number that, if this works, will keep climbing until it cannot be ignored.
