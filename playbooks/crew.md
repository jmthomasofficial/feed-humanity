# Tier 2: The Crew — $50 to $200, 1 to 3 Hours

You and three to five friends want to make a day of it. A Feed Run is a planned multi-stop route through your city, bringing bulk food to people who need it. This is where the content gets genuinely compelling — a mini-documentary of your crew moving through the city, making real things happen at real locations.

---

## The Complete Checklist

- [ ] **Assemble your crew.** Three to five people is the sweet spot. One person handles logistics and purchases, one person films, everyone else carries and distributes. Roles do not need to be rigid, but naming them beforehand prevents chaos.

- [ ] **Plan your Feed Run route.** Pick three to five stops where people need food. (See route planning section below.) Write out the stops in order before you leave. Factor in travel time — a good run is two to three hours including transit.

- [ ] **Buy your food in bulk.** (See bulk buying guide below.) Aim for 10 to 25 meals depending on your budget. Quantity matters here — the visual of 20 meals going out in one run is more powerful than one meal five times.

- [ ] **Do the run.** Move stop to stop. Hand out the food. Be present with people — don't rush the moment just to get the shot. Let the conversations happen. Those moments are the content.

- [ ] **Document the whole run.** One person films continuously. Capture the buying moment, the car loaded with food, each stop, the reactions, the empty bags at the end. You have enough for a 2-minute mini-doc and 4 to 6 standalone clips.

- [ ] **Post the run.** Options: full mini-doc on YouTube/TikTok, individual stop clips as a series, before/after (loaded car vs. empty bags), a time-lapse montage. Use `#FeedHumanity` + your city on all of it.

- [ ] **Tag the food spots that helped.** If a restaurant donated, tag them. If Costco or Sam's Club was involved, tag them. These businesses see that tag and it becomes social proof for the next partnership.

- [ ] **Challenge three other friend groups.** Not just individuals — tag other crews. "Hey @[crew/group] — do this in your city." Group challenges spread faster than individual ones.

- [ ] **Share your route template.** Post the actual route (neighborhoods, types of stops, what you bought) so other crews in your city can replicate it without starting from scratch. A replicable route is worth ten times more than a single documented run.

## Feed Run Route Planning

### Who to Find

**Job sites and construction zones.**
Road crews, building construction workers, landscapers, utility workers — they work physically demanding jobs, often starting before dawn, frequently in weather that makes everything harder. They almost never receive random acts of kindness. The reaction you get from a construction crew is some of the most genuine footage you will ever capture. Show up with 20 sandwiches and watch what happens.

How to find them: Drive the commercial zones of your city in the morning. Construction sites are visible from the street. Road crews are listed in local traffic advisories. You can also call your city's public works department and ask if there are any active road projects — they will tell you.

**Parks where unhoused people gather.**
City parks, river walks, underpasses, bus terminals. You likely know where these spots are in your city. If you do not, ask anyone who works at a local shelter — they know every spot.

Tip: Bring food that does not require utensils, does not need to be heated, and travels well. Wrapped sandwiches, sealed snack bags, bottled water or juice, fruit that does not bruise easily. Avoid anything messy or fragile.

**Overnight shift workers.**
Hospitals (the staff entrance at shift change, not the patients), factories, warehouses, 24-hour stores, hotel staff entrances, truck stops. The 11 PM to 7 AM shift is when food options are worst and energy is lowest. Showing up at 2 AM with food for the overnight crew at a distribution center is something they will remember and talk about.

**Apartment complexes in underserved areas.**
Knock on the property manager's door first and explain what you are doing. Most will let you set up in the parking lot or common area. You can reach 20 to 50 families in a single stop if you bring enough.

**Transition housing and halfway houses.**
Call ahead — these facilities have rules about outside visitors. Ask the house manager what they need most and what time works. These stops tend to generate the most emotionally resonant content because people are in real transition and genuine appreciation comes through.

### Building the Route

Order your stops to minimize travel time. Put the heaviest/most food-intensive stops first (when you have full bags) and lighter stops toward the end. If you're carrying hot food, plan the route so hot food is distributed first.

Budget 20 to 30 minutes per stop for distribution and filming. A 5-stop run will take 2 to 3 hours including transit. Do not over-schedule — running out of food at stop 3 of 5 is demoralizing. Better to finish strong with abundance than arrive empty at the last stop.

## Bulk Buying Guide

### Costco

Costco is the best value for Feed Runs. A $100 Costco run yields approximately 20 to 30 complete meals depending on what you buy.

High-value items for a Feed Run:
- **Rotisserie chickens** ($5.99 each, feeds 3-4 people): Buy 6 to 8, divide into portions in aluminum foil trays. Warm, real food.
- **Sandwich platters** ($19.99, serves 12-16): Ready to distribute, no prep required.
- **Bottled water cases** ($4.99 per 40-count): Always needed, universally welcome.
- **Protein bar boxes** ($18.99, 48 bars): Non-perishable, easy to distribute.
- **Canned goods pallets**: If delivering to a food bank, the large canned goods packs go further than any prepared food.
- **Bread loaves, peanut butter jars, jelly jars**: $35 total buys enough for approximately 60 sandwiches.

### Sam's Club

Similar to Costco. Key differences:
- More locations in certain regions
- Deli counter can prepare large orders with advance notice (call the day before)
- "Grab and Go" prepared foods section has ready-made options

### Restaurant Partnerships

Call the day before. Ask for the manager. Say: "We're doing a community Feed Run tomorrow for the Feed Humanity campaign and we'd like to buy 20 to 30 meals in bulk. Can you give us a group discount or donate any surplus?" Most local restaurants will either discount or donate when asked directly. Chain locations can sometimes participate via their corporate giving programs — worth a call.

## Documentation Tips

**One phone. One person. Full commitment.**
Designate a dedicated filmer before you leave. This person's only job is to document. They do not carry bags, do not distribute, do not get distracted. Full attention on capturing the moments.

**What to capture:**
- The store run: piling the cart, loading the car, the receipt total (shows the scale of what you spent)
- The driving between stops: the crew in the car, the anticipation
- Each arrival: getting out, approaching people, the first contact
- The distribution itself: food changing hands, faces, reactions
- Conversations if people are open to it — even 15 seconds of genuine exchange is powerful
- The empty bags at the end: this is your visual proof of completion

**Vertical format for everything.** TikTok and Reels are where this content performs best. Film everything vertically, or at minimum square format. Horizontal footage can be cropped but you lose resolution.

**Get natural audio.** Wind, traffic, conversation, ambient city sound — this is what makes documentary content feel real. Do not try to be silent. The context noise is the atmosphere.

**Edit as a series.** Five 30-second clips, one per stop, posted across one day, each one challenging the next neighborhood or the next crew — this approach outperforms a single 3-minute video every time.

After your run, post this information publicly (in your caption, in a comment, or on the Feed Humanity community board):

```
CITY: [your city]
DATE: [when you ran this route]
CREW SIZE: [number of people]
BUDGET: [$X spent / Y meals delivered]

STOP 1: [neighborhood/type of location] — [what you brought] — [reception notes]
STOP 2: [neighborhood/type of location] — [what you brought] — [reception notes]
STOP 3: [neighborhood/type of location] — [what you brought] — [reception notes]

WHERE WE BOUGHT: [specific stores, any partnerships]
TOTAL MEALS: [your count]
TIPS FOR REPLICATION: [anything future crews should know]
```

This template is how a single run becomes a replicable system. The first crew in Memphis that shares their route enables the second crew to do it better and faster. That second crew shares their improvements. By the third iteration, the route is optimized. Scale is just iteration.
