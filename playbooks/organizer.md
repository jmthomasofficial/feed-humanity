# Tier 3: The Organizer — $0 to $500, 1 Week Prep

You want to lead something real in your community but you do not know where to start. This playbook is a complete, step-by-step event guide. Follow it and you will run a legitimate food drive that delivers measurable results to a real food bank.

No event experience required. No budget required to start. The only hard requirement is one week of lead time.

---

## The Complete Checklist

- [ ] Find your nearest food bank and make contact
- [ ] Choose a collection point
- [ ] Download the Feed Humanity Event Kit and prep your materials
- [ ] Set your date and start promotion (5 to 7 days out)
- [ ] Run the event
- [ ] Document and deliver
- [ ] Post the full story and challenge three other organizers

## Step 1: Find and Call Your Nearest Food Bank

### Finding the right food bank

The AI locator at [jmthomasofficial.com/feedhumanity](https://jmthomasofficial.com/feedhumanity) will show you food banks near your zip code. Alternatively:

- **Feeding America network locator**: feedingamerica.org/find-your-local-foodbank
- **Google**: Search "food bank near [your city]" — most are prominently listed
- **211.org**: Dial 2-1-1 in the US for local social services referrals

Start with the closest one. Call their main number and ask for the volunteer coordinator or donations manager.

### The call script

"Hi, my name is [your name]. I am organizing a community food drive for the Feed Humanity campaign. I want to donate everything we collect directly to you. Can you tell me what items you need most right now, and what your intake process looks like?"

Then listen. They will tell you exactly what to collect, whether they have specific needs (baby formula, hygiene items, specific canned goods), their intake hours, and whether they want you to pre-sort donations. Write this down. This call takes 5 minutes and it is the single most important step in the whole process.

### Why call instead of just showing up with stuff

Food banks have intake windows, storage capacity limits, and frequently changing priority needs. A food drive that collects 500 cans of beets when the food bank is overstocked on beets and desperate for cooking oil is a waste of everyone's effort. One phone call ensures every item you collect has maximum impact.

## Step 2: Choose a Collection Point

Your collection point is where donors bring food during your drive. It should be somewhere with foot traffic and easy access.

**Best options:**
- Your office lobby or break room (captive audience, built-in social pressure)
- A church, mosque, temple, or community center (existing congregation infrastructure)
- A school entrance or library lobby (high daily foot traffic)
- Your apartment complex common area or mailbox cluster
- A local business whose owner is willing to participate (set up a box near the register)
- Your front porch or driveway for a neighborhood drive

**What you need at the collection point:**
- A large, visible box or bin clearly labeled with the #FeedHumanity poster (downloadable from the event kit)
- A printed list of accepted and most-needed items (the food bank will tell you)
- A QR code linking to the campaign website (downloadable from the event kit)

You can have multiple collection points. Coordinate with friends in different offices or neighborhoods to run simultaneous drives with the same food bank as the destination.

## 7-Day Promotion Timeline

### 7 Days Out: Launch

- Post your announcement on all your personal social channels. Use the caption template from the event kit. Include: what you're doing, where the collection point is, what items are needed, the date the collection ends.
- Send the email template to your workplace, neighborhood, or congregation.
- Put up posters at the collection point and any nearby bulletin boards.
- Ask two or three people you trust to share your post. Personal asks get 3x more shares than passive "please share" appeals.

### 5 Days Out: Proof of Concept

- Post a photo of your collection box — even if it only has a few items in it. "Day 2 — it's starting." Progress photos generate participation. People want to contribute to something already moving.
- Share the food bank's specific wishlist one more time. "They specifically asked for cooking oil and canned protein" is more actionable than "donate non-perishables."

### 3 Days Out: Urgency Push

- Send a follow-up email with the current count and the deadline.
- Post a video asking people directly to participate. Standing next to your half-full collection box and speaking to camera is more effective than any graphic.
- Tag local accounts — neighborhood Facebook groups, local news, community organizations.

### 1 Day Out: Final Push

- "Tomorrow is the last day." Post this everywhere. Final-day urgency drives disproportionate donations. Many people who intended to participate have forgotten. This reminder converts them.
- Confirm your delivery logistics with the food bank — arrival time, where to bring the donation, who to ask for.

## Day-of Logistics Checklist

**Morning of (before the collection window closes):**
- Photograph the full collection box. "Before" documentation is critical.
- Text the food bank to confirm your arrival time.
- Gather boxes, bags, and any volunteers helping with transport.

**During the event (if running a live collection event, not a drop-off drive):**
- Set up before attendees arrive. Collection box visible at the entrance.
- Greet everyone who donates. Say thank you personally. This is what makes people come back.
- Keep a running count. You will want this number for your post.
- Film the box filling up if possible. Time-lapses of donation boxes filling are reliable content.

**After the collection closes:**
- Count and photograph the full haul before boxing it up.
- Sort into food bank's preferred categories if they asked for pre-sorting.
- Load everything up and head to the food bank.

**At the food bank:**
- Bring a helper if you can — intake can involve carrying heavy boxes.
- Ask for the volunteer coordinator by name (the person you called earlier).
- Have your camera ready. Film yourself walking in with the donation. Film the intake process if the staff is comfortable with it. Get their reaction on camera — food bank staff are genuinely moved by community donations and that authenticity shows.
- Ask for a receipt or written acknowledgment of the donation weight/count. This is your official impact number.

## Post-Event Documentation Guide

### Immediately After Delivery

Post to all platforms within 4 hours of delivery while the emotion is fresh. Your post should include:

1. The "before" image (empty or sparse collection box)
2. The "after" image (overflowing collection box or the haul laid out)
3. The handoff — you carrying donations into the food bank
4. The official count: "X pounds of food / Y meals delivered to [Food Bank Name]"

Caption formula that works:
"[City] showed up. We collected [count] for [food bank] in [X days]. Every single item goes directly to families in [neighborhood/city]. [Tag food bank] [Tag 3 people you're challenging next] #FeedHumanity #FeedHumanity[City]"

### What to Tag

- Tag the food bank's social accounts in your post. They will almost always reshare this.
- Tag the businesses that hosted collection points.
- Tag anyone who contributed significantly.
- Challenge three other organizers by name — friends who run community groups, church leaders, office managers, HOA board members. "Your turn."

### The Story Format (Most Powerful)

If you can, post a full story: empty box on day one, gradual fill, overflow at the end, the drive to the food bank, the handoff, the receipt. Even a rough, low-production cut of this performs well because the narrative arc is undeniable. Rising action, climax, resolution. It is the structure every human brain is wired to respond to.

### Share What You Learned

Post a follow-up "lessons from the run" either in a comment or a separate post. What worked, what would you do differently, who in your community showed up most. This content helps the next organizer do it better. That replication is the whole point.

## Emergency Protocols

**Low turnout or sparse donations:**
Do not cancel. Do not post about disappointment. Deliver what you have — even 10 cans of soup is 10 meals for someone. Post the story honestly: "We're just getting started. Next drive will be bigger." Small, consistent, real actions compound. The first food drive is always the smallest. The second is always bigger.

**Weather cancellation:**
If you have a live outdoor event and weather makes it impossible, shift to a drop-off only model. Post immediately that the live event is cancelled but the collection box stays through the original end date. Rain does not stop a collection box from sitting in an office lobby.

**Donation overflow:**
A good problem to have. Call the food bank ahead of time if you can see you're running over capacity. Many food banks can direct surplus to a partner organization. In a pinch, most food banks have overflow procedures — they have seen this before.

**Food bank at capacity:**
If your primary contact cannot accept your donation on the scheduled day, have a backup. Most communities have multiple food organizations. Feeding America's website lists all partner organizations. A homeless shelter, a soup kitchen, or a transitional housing facility can almost always accept non-perishable food.

## The Bigger Picture

Every Tier 3 organizer who runs a real food drive and shares the story with measurable results adds a data point that cannot be argued with. Not a hashtag — a receipt from a food bank with a pound count. Not a caption — footage of actual food being carried through actual doors.

The campaign lives or dies on the reality of these moments. Your job is to make one of those moments real.
