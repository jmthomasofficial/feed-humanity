# Tier 4: The Business — $200 to $2,000

You run a restaurant, cafe, retail shop, service business, or local chain. This playbook is for you. Participating at this tier does not require a large budget, a PR team, or a complicated campaign. It requires picking one mechanic, activating it, and posting about it.

The businesses that move first in their neighborhood become the visible leaders of local community care. That social position is worth far more than the cost of the food.

---

## Pick Your Mechanic

### For Restaurants and Cafes

**Buy One, Give One**

Every purchase triggers a donated meal. The execution is simple: when a customer orders anything, a pre-made meal (usually the most efficient item on your menu for cost per calorie) goes into a designated "Give Box" at the counter. At closing, a volunteer or staff member delivers the box to a pre-arranged food bank, shelter, or community meal program.

Setup:
1. Identify your cheapest, most calorie-efficient item — rice bowls, soups, wraps.
2. Designate a shelf or cooler space for the Give Box.
3. Call your nearest food bank or shelter and ask when they accept fresh food donations.
4. Confirm you can deliver within 2 hours of closing (most will accommodate).
5. Put up the #FeedHumanity poster at the register. Train staff to mention it during ordering.
6. Run it for one day. Track how many meals went out. Post the count.

Content angle: Film the Give Box filling up throughout the day. Time-lapse from morning prep through the evening rush to the final count. Then film the delivery. This is a complete story arc in under 3 minutes.

**Surplus Donation at Closing**

Instead of trashing end-of-day surplus, route it to the nearest shelter or community meal program. In the US, restaurants discard between 22 and 33 percent of what they purchase. Even a small restaurant generates enough surplus food to feed 5 to 20 people per night if it is redirected instead of discarded.

Setup:
1. Register your restaurant in the AI Dispatch system at [jmthomasofficial.com/feedhumanity](https://jmthomasofficial.com/feedhumanity).
2. Each evening, enter your surplus: what you have, quantity, and your pickup window.
3. The dispatch system matches you to the nearest recipient and sends them a notification.
4. They come, pick up, done.

This becomes a permanent operational change, not a one-time campaign. The food that was going in your trash now feeds people. Tax deduction documentation is available through most food rescue organizations — a lawyer can confirm the applicable deduction for your region.

**The Community Table**

Reserve one table per shift for anyone who cannot pay. No voucher required. No application process. No proof of need. Just a table with a small #FeedHumanity sign on it that says: "This table is for anyone who needs it today."

How it works in practice:
- Post about it once when you launch it: "We have a Community Table. If you need a meal today, it's on us. No questions."
- The table may sit empty most shifts. On the days someone sits there, you feed a person who needed it.
- The act of having the table changes the culture of your space. Staff take pride in it. Regulars tip a little more because they know where some of it goes.

The Community Table story tells itself. You post the table. Occasionally something genuinely moving happens at it. You post that. It becomes part of your brand identity in a way that no marketing campaign can manufacture.

### For Retail and Service Businesses

**Fill the Box**

Place a collection box near your register for non-perishable food donations. Partner with a local food bank (one call to arrange pickup or a monthly delivery). Put up the #FeedHumanity poster next to it.

Execution details:
- Box should be large, visible, and clearly labeled. A plain cardboard box with a handwritten sign works. A branded container with the #FeedHumanity logo is better.
- Post a specific wishlist from your food bank partner — "they need cooking oil and canned protein most right now" — on a card next to the box.
- Empty the box and post the count every 1 to 2 weeks.

The box does the work. Your job is to make it visible and let your customers do the rest. Many customers want to help but never have a convenient mechanism. You become the mechanism.

**Match Customer Donations at Checkout**

"Would you like to round up for Feed Humanity?" or "For $1 at checkout, we'll match it and donate both dollars to [local food bank]."

If your point-of-sale system supports donation prompts, this is extremely low friction. If it does not, a physical jar at the register works. At the end of each week, tally the jar and match it yourself. Post the weekly total.

Content angle: "This week our customers donated $87. We matched it. $174 goes to [food bank name] tomorrow."

**Sponsor a Local Feed Run**

Pay for the food. Let a Tier 2 crew do the distribution. Your logo and business name appear in their post, at the start of their run, on their bags if you can provide them.

This is the most efficient PR play in this playbook. You spend $100 to $200 on bulk food, a crew of five people distributes it across your city, the content gets shared to five separate audiences, and your business name is in every caption.

## Social Media Angles

Business participation generates distinct content types that perform well:

**The announcement**: "Starting [date], every purchase at [business name] triggers a donated meal. Here's how it works." Short, clear, specific. People like knowing the mechanic.

**The operation behind the scenes**: Kitchen prep for the Give Box, sorting donations after closing, loading the surplus for pickup. Behind-the-scenes food service content already performs well — now it has mission attached.

**The count**: Weekly or daily "here's what we did" posts. "Today: 47 meals donated." Simple numbers, real impact. Consistency builds audience trust faster than anything else.

**Staff participation**: Your staff doing the challenge individually, on their own phones, tagging the business. Employee advocacy content outperforms brand content on most platforms. Encourage it, do not mandate it.

**The challenge to other local businesses**: "We're in. [Business down the street], your turn." Local business-to-business challenges spread through neighborhood business networks extremely effectively. Your city's business community likely has a Facebook group or a Chamber of Commerce newsletter. This is how the mechanic reaches people who are not following the main campaign.

Download the #FeedHumanity poster from the event kit. Print it. Put it up. A visible, physical poster at your point of sale:
- Tells every customer that your business participates
- Invites customers to participate themselves
- Creates a conversation starter for staff

The poster is free. The printing costs a dollar. The signal it sends to your community is worth considerably more than that.

## Challenge Three Other Businesses

This is the mechanic that makes participation spread beyond a single business. After your first post, directly tag three other local businesses: "We're in. @[Business1] @[Business2] @[Business3] — your turn. #FeedHumanity"

The public nomination creates gentle social pressure. Most businesses will not publicly decline to feed people when challenged in front of their customer base. The ones that accept generate their own content, tag more businesses, and the chain continues.

Tag businesses that are:
- Your friendly competitors (they want to match you)
- Businesses your customers also visit (shared audience)
- Businesses owned by people you know personally (direct social connection converts better than cold tags)
