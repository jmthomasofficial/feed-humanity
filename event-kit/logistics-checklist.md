# Feed Humanity Event — Day-of Operations Guide

This is the complete logistics checklist for Tier 3 organizers running a food drive or community Feed event. Print this out. Work through it in order. Check each box as you complete it.

---

## Pre-Event: One Week Before

- [ ] **Confirm with your food bank.** Call your contact again to confirm: accepted items, intake hours, drop-off location, whether they want pre-sorted donations. Ask if anything on their needs list has changed since your first call.
- [ ] **Finalize your collection point.** Confirm you have permission to place a collection box at each location. Get a contact name at each site.
- [ ] **Print and post all materials.** Posters at each collection point. Flyers on any bulletin boards, break room tables, community boards. QR codes pointing to the campaign website.
- [ ] **Send your main announcement.** Use the email template from the event kit. Send to your workplace, neighborhood list, church/congregation, or any group you're organizing. Include: event dates, collection point locations, needed items, and the food bank you're benefiting.
- [ ] **Line up a delivery helper.** You will be carrying boxes on delivery day. Identify at least one person who can help you load and unload. Confirm their availability.
- [ ] **Source extra boxes and bags.** Visit a grocery store or liquor store and ask for empty boxes. Have more than you think you will need. Overfull boxes break. Multiple smaller boxes are easier to carry and stack.
- [ ] **Test your documentation setup.** Make sure your phone has enough storage and charge. Know where you will charge it the day before the event.

---

## Pre-Event: One Day Before

- [ ] **Post the final urgency reminder.** "Tomorrow is the last day to donate." Include the collection point location, hours, and the specific items the food bank needs most. This post converts the people who intended to participate but forgot.
- [ ] **Confirm your delivery plan.** Reconfirm with your food bank contact: what time you will arrive, where the intake entrance is, who to ask for, whether you need to bring ID or documentation.
- [ ] **Prepare your collection containers.** Set out fresh boxes or bins at each collection point if any are getting full. Bring extra bags and tape.
- [ ] **Charge all devices.** Phone, backup battery pack, anything you are using to document the event.
- [ ] **Confirm your delivery vehicle.** If you are moving a large haul, make sure the vehicle is available. If you do not have a vehicle large enough, arrange a truck rental or ask a friend with an SUV or van. This is the step most organizers forget until it is too late.
- [ ] **Take the "before" photos.** Photograph each collection point as it stands right now — including empty or sparse boxes. These before photos are essential for the post-event story.

---

## Pre-Event: Morning of Event Day

- [ ] **Arrive at collection points early.** Check the status of each box before attendees arrive if you are running a live event. If a box is nearly full, move items to a backup storage area and reset it.
- [ ] **Set up your event space** (if running a live event rather than a passive drop-off drive):
  - Collection box at the entrance, clearly visible
  - #FeedHumanity poster prominently displayed
  - Printed wishlist of most-needed items posted next to the box
  - A volunteer stationed at the entrance to greet donors
- [ ] **Brief your volunteers.** Everyone should know: where to direct donors, what the event is about (one sentence), how to count and record donations, and who to call if something goes wrong.
- [ ] **Set up your documentation station.** Designate one person to film throughout the event. Pre-plan two or three specific shots: the entrance as people arrive, the box filling up, donor faces (only with consent), the final count.

---

## During the Event

- [ ] **Greet every donor personally.** Say thank you. Make eye contact. This is the moment that determines whether they come back next year. The experience of being personally thanked for a contribution is what turns a one-time donor into a recurring one.
- [ ] **Keep a running count.** Have a notepad or your phone's notes app open. Every time something goes in the box, note it. You want a real number at the end, not a guess.
- [ ] **Document throughout — not just at the end.** The narrative of a gradually filling box is more powerful than a single full-box photo. Capture early, middle, and late in the collection window.
- [ ] **Manage overflow.** If a collection box gets completely full, move contents to a sealed storage area and reset with a fresh box. Label full boxes immediately so the count stays accurate.
- [ ] **Respond to questions from donors.** Common questions: "Where does this go?" → Name the food bank. "What do they need most?" → Read from the printed wishlist. "Can I donate money instead?" → Yes; direct them to the food bank's donation page or accept cash to purchase items yourself.
- [ ] **Handle unexpected surplus.** If you receive more than expected and your primary food bank's intake is at capacity: call your backup organization (shelter, soup kitchen, another food bank). Have their number ready in your phone.

---

## Post-Event: Teardown

- [ ] **Count everything before you move it.** Count total items, estimate total weight if possible. Write the number down. This is your impact metric.
- [ ] **Photograph the final haul.** Every box, bag, and item laid out or stacked. This is your "after" photo. The contrast between this and your "before" photo is your post-event story.
- [ ] **Thank your volunteers in person.** Before anyone leaves. They gave you their time. They should hear it mattered.
- [ ] **Pack everything securely for transport.** Heavy canned goods in smaller boxes. Fragile items padded. Nothing loose in the vehicle. Label boxes if the food bank asked for pre-sorting by category.
- [ ] **Clean up the collection point.** Leave every location better than you found it. Remove posters from walls if they were taped directly. Collect any scraps or debris. If you used a business's lobby, thank the owner or manager in person before you leave.

---

## Post-Event: Delivery to the Food Bank

- [ ] **Arrive at the scheduled time.** Call ahead if you are running more than 15 minutes late. Food bank intake staff have schedules and waiting volunteers. Be respectful of their time.
- [ ] **Ask for your food bank contact by name.** The person you called during planning. This continuity matters — it builds a relationship for the next event.
- [ ] **Carry everything in together.** Do not leave items in the car to retrieve later. Bring your delivery helper and move everything in a single trip if possible.
- [ ] **Get your receipt or acknowledgment.** Ask: "Can you give me a receipt or a note showing the weight or item count we donated?" Most food banks have a standard intake form. This is your official impact documentation. Keep a photo of it.
- [ ] **Film the handoff.** You walking in with the donation. The food bank staff receiving it. Their reaction, if they are comfortable. This is the climax of your story arc. Even 30 seconds of this footage is powerful.
- [ ] **Ask if they need anything else.** "Is there anything else we can help with today?" Sometimes there is a task — sorting donations already received, stacking boxes, loading distribution vehicles. Thirty minutes of extra help from your crew is meaningful.

---

## Post-Event: Social Posting

Post within 4 hours of delivery. The emotional immediacy matters. Use all of the following:

- [ ] **Side-by-side: empty box vs. full haul.** The most effective single image in the entire event story.
- [ ] **The delivery handoff clip.** Even rough footage is effective here. The food bank environment is the context.
- [ ] **The impact number.** "X pounds / Y meals delivered to [Food Bank Name] today." Specific numbers. The food bank's official receipt number if you have it.
- [ ] **Tag the food bank.** Almost every food bank has social media accounts. They will reshare your post. Their audience is exactly the people you want to reach.
- [ ] **Tag anyone who helped.** Volunteers, donors who consented to being tagged, businesses that hosted collection points.
- [ ] **Challenge three other organizers.** Not generic followers — name specific people who you believe can run the next event. "@[Name1] @[Name2] @[Name3] — your community needs this. Here's how we did it: [link to this repo]"
- [ ] **Post your lessons.** A short follow-up post: what worked, what you would do differently, what surprised you. This is the content that the next organizer needs. Your experience is infrastructure for them.

## Emergency Protocols

### Weather Forces Cancellation of a Live Event

Post immediately that the live event is postponed. Announce the new date if you have one. Make clear that the drop-off collection box remains in place through the original end date. Most donations to a passive drop-off drive happen regardless of weather — it is only in-person events that weather disrupts.

### Low Turnout

Do not cancel, and do not post about disappointment. Deliver whatever you have collected. Even 15 cans of food is 15 cans that were not there before. Post the outcome honestly: "First event — small but real. Next one will be bigger." First events are always the smallest. Consistency matters more than scale in the early stages. The second event is always better, because you know what you are doing.

### Donation Box Theft or Vandalism

Document it (photo), report it to building management or police if appropriate, replace the box, and do not make it a public story. One incident does not end the event. Keep going.

### Surplus Beyond Capacity

A genuinely good problem. Steps in order:
1. Call your food bank contact — they often have partner organizations who can accept overflow.
2. Call a local shelter or soup kitchen directly.
3. Post in the Feed Humanity community board asking if any other local organizers can take the surplus.
4. In a true emergency, check if Feeding America's partner locator shows another nearby organization.

Never let surplus food go to waste if there is any alternative. If every option is exhausted, document the attempt and learn for next time.

### Volunteer No-Shows

If your dedicated filmer does not arrive, designate any volunteer to film on their phone. Imperfect documentation is better than no documentation. A shaky phone video of a real food handoff is more valuable content than polished footage that never happened. If you are running solo, prop your phone against something and film yourself working. It is fine.

## Metrics to Record

Keep these numbers from every event. They tell the story of your impact over time and help future organizers calibrate their events.

- Date and location
- Collection window duration (days)
- Number of collection points
- Number of volunteers
- Total items collected (count)
- Estimated total weight (lbs or kg)
- Estimated meal equivalents (the food bank can often give you this number)
- Food bank name and contact
- Total social reach (sum of views/impressions across your posts)
- Number of new participants who joined the campaign because of your event

These numbers go into the `data/` directory of this repo as your city's contribution to the global dataset. They are also your record for doing this again next quarter.
